Mission and values

The Monday Charitable Trust (MCT) is a grantmaking foundation, whose key thematic areas for providing charitable support are Housing, Education and Environmental Crises, underpinned by secondary themes of Young People, Mental Health and Social Mobility and Inequality. The Charity aims to improve life chances of disadvantaged and often marginalised groups in the United Kingdom, while providing support to charities tackling environmental crises.

Our funding

We fund programmes which address or seek to identify the root causes of issues. We regard the Charity as a long-term funder which is able to be flexible and independent, willing to support innovative ideas for tackling problems, as we believe that such programmes will deliver the greatest social impact over the long term.

Our history

Year 1932

Chelsea Estates Limited

Chelsea Estates Limited is an integral part of our history. It was incorporated on 18 April 1932 and had a long and successful history as a property investment company. It was founded by John Munday and was carried on after his death first by his son, Reginald, and then by his son-in-law, Harold Lane. After Harold Lane’s death, the company was run by his son, Robert Lane.

Year 1972

Ownership passes to Robert Lane

Under Robert Lane’s stewardship, the company was transformed. It moved away from investing in residential property and into a wide range of commercial property – in the retail, industrial and office sectors.

Year 2008

Robert and Elspeth Lane

MCT is the successor charity to a charitable trust of the same name which was created by Robert and Elspeth Lane in January 2008

Year 2013

Charitable Trust

In 2013, Robert Lane made the very generous gift of all the shares in Chelsea Estates Limited to the charitable trust he and Elspeth had created in 2008. As a result, ownership of the company passed to the trust.

Year 2017

Monday Charitable Trust

MTC was itself formed in 2017, as a precursor to a reorganisation which took place the following year, in which the assets of the predecessor charitable trust and its subsidiary, Chelsea Estates Limited, were transferred to it.

Year 2018

Grant to the Royal College of Art

In 2018, the Trustees agreed to make a capital grant of £1m to the Royal College of Art to mark the long relationship between Chelsea Estates and the College, which for many years occupied one of the Company’s properties in Battersea. The grant has been applied towards the creation of a state-of-the-art building on the Battersea site for postgraduate students and entrepreneurs, delivering new studios, workshops and incubator units to support the College's future.

Year 2022

New campus

In May 2022, The Monday Charity Library and Lane Library Terrace were opened as key areas in the Royal College of Art’s new Battersea campus, forming the heart of its collaborative and social spaces.

A tribute to our founder Robert Lane

Robert Lane was born in 1932 in London. His prep school in Chichester was evacuated to Cornwall during the war. He went to Sherborne School and afterwards did his two years’ National Service in the Royal Tank Regiment.

Both sides of his family were involved in the transport and haulage business. His grandfather’s firm, J R Munday Limited, had major haulage contracts for London County Council. The family’s property company, Chelsea Estates Limited, owned land in Battersea which was, for most of the 20th century, a disregarded area of London. Robert’s grandfather bought the land now occupied by the Royal College of Art by Battersea Bridge. Robert joined the family business in the late 1950s and worked all his life to manage and enhance it. He became the sole owner on the death of his father, Harold Lane, in 1972.

Over the years Robert enhanced the portfolio of properties that Chelsea Estates owned in the South of England. He oversaw the change from residential to commercial properties and started to carry out development of new sites. As a young man, Robert bought a small piece of land beside the railway bridge next to the Thames to park lorries. Robert could not have guessed at the time that, in 2012, this site would be sold by Chelsea Estates for £24 million and become the site for a 33 storey block of desirable flats.

Robert and his wife Elspeth were extraordinarily generous. When the time came for Robert to relinquish the burden of running Chelsea Estates in 2012, instead of extracting their value from the company, he and Elspeth decided to give their shareholdings to a charitable trust, also named the Monday Charitable Trust, which they had established a few years before. The charity has since become an incorporated charity, whose aim is to improve the life chances of people who suffer from disadvantages: homelessness, mental health problems, lack of education and poor social mobility.

Robert was a kind, courteous and modest man, always careful in his business dealings. He never sought publicity for his generosity. Outside the business, as a young man he loved sailing and had a boat on the South Coast. He and Elspeth met while skiing in Switzerland and they also shared a love of racing. They visited Scotland regularly, enjoying cruises round the Scottish islands. They went to Cornwall each year and cream teas were a particular favourite of Robert’s.

Elspeth Lane

Elspeth Lane

Founder Trustee

Elspeth was born in Singapore and managed to leave before the fall of Singapore in February 1942. She was too young to remember the journey on the high seas back to Britain. Her father was a civilian prisoner in Singapore for three and a half years at that time. She was educated and lived in Scotland. Before meeting Robert, she had worked part time as a secretary and also part time as the village postman, providing cover during the various postmen’s holidays. She spent some of her time with her two horses winning the Small Hunter Class and the Working Hunter Class at the Royal Highland Show in Edinburgh and going to horse trials around the country. She met Robert in 1973, and they married in 1977. Elspeth moved South to farm on the Surrey/Hampshire border. She was very much involved in the running of the dairy herd, and the arable crops. Interests include ski-ing (in her younger days), travel and horse racing.

Trust structure

MCT is a charitable company limited by guarantee, incorporated on 17 August 2017 (company registration number 10910936) and registered with the Charity Commissioners as a charity (charity registration number 1174232). MCT’s governing document is the Articles of Association dated 17 August 2017, as amended by Special Resolution dated 23 April 2019.

The minimum number of trustees required by the Articles is three and the maximum is eight. Robert and Elspeth Lane, Jonathan Brinsden and Sarah Baxter were the four original trustees. They became Founder Trustees and serve an indefinite term based on their relative experience and contribution to MCT as a whole.

New trustees are Elected Trustees and serve a term of three years. They are appointed by ordinary resolution and may serve a maximum of two terms. They may thereafter be reappointed annually if recommended by the chairman.

Management committees

The Trustees have delegated certain responsibilities to three Committees.

The Management and Operations (Ops) Committee reviews the financial and governance arrangements for the Charity. The Ops Committee is responsible for reporting to the Trustees on investment and property matters and directing the investment managers and property advisers as appropriate.

The Social Investments Committee (the Tuesday Committee) reviews Social Investment opportunities, obtains advice on them as needed and then advises the Trustees on the level of investment and terms.

The Investment Committee has responsibility in connection with the benchmarking of investment performance, adherence to the Charity’s investment policy and to support the development of the Charity’s overall investment strategy and aims regarding the Charity’s quoted and unquoted investments.

Investment policy

In 2024, in a review of the Charity’s investment strategy and requirements, the Trustees considered various approaches to sustainable investment in the context of the Spectrum of Capital Model (below) and the FCA’s Sustainability Disclosure Requirements (SDR), as well as climate change.

The Spectrum of Capital Model

Source: Bridges Impact + and the Impact Management Project

The Spectrum of Capital is a way of mapping the broad range of risk/return strategies in sustainable and social impact investing, from investing for maximum profit with no regard to impact through to philanthropic investment with potential loss of capital. This is overlaid with Impact Management’s ABC model: Avoid Harm / Benefit Stakeholders / Contribute to Solutions, which seeks to further classify investments focusing on their impact on people and the planet.

In addition to the spectrum of capital framework, some investment managers aim to align with the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) to identify areas where they can engage with companies to encourage best practice, as well as areas where they can identify investment opportunities.

Following a selection process undertaken by an independent consultancy firm, the Trustees appointed CCLA and Sarasin & Partners as their principal investment managers on a fully delegated basis. The Trustees have agreed an investment policy and have asked both CCLA and Sarasin & Partners to invest on a total return basis with income reinvested. A benchmark of CPI + 4% will be applied to the investment portfolio.

In addition, the Trustees decided that up to 15% of the Charity’s net assets would be allocated to Social Investments and Impact Investments, both of which would be focused on the three thematic areas of the Charity - Housing, Education and Environmental Crises.


Environmental, social and governance (ESG) factors

The expectation of the Trustees is that the companies selected for investment and managers of the funds appointed by CCLA and Sarasin will exclude those with poor ESG ratings, allowing for engagement with fund managers to improve ESG ratings. The Charity’s Investment Committee will periodically discuss and review the ESG ratings with CCLA and Sarasin as part of their monitoring role.

The Trustees have requested CCLA and Sarasin to be proactive on their behalf by encouraging the companies selected for investment and managers of the funds appointed by them to engage on issues that are aligned with their impact goals, focusing on ESG factors and exercising their voting rights on the Charity’s behalf to encourage better corporate governance. CCLA and Sarasin will continue reporting, monitoring and challenging the company and fund managers on their ESG performance.
